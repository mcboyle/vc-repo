# VeraCrypt 1.26.29 Hardened Fork — External Claims Verification, Pass 2

## TL;DR
- **Load-bearing item A (ML-KEM license) is CONFIRMED**: the US-Portfolio license contains a §2.9 clause stating verbatim that "any modification, extension, or derivation of the parameters of the PQC ALGORITHM, is not an implementation or use of the PQC algorithm" — so altering ML-KEM parameters exits the royalty-free safe harbor. **The fork must NOT alter ML-KEM parameters.**
- **Load-bearing item B (HIVE/DataLair figures) is CONFIRMED**: DataLair PoPETs 2017 Table 1 reports hidden-write 2.92 MB/s, dm-crypt public-write 210.10 MB/s, HIVE hidden-write 0.60 MB/s; both claimed breaks (Paterson–Strefler RC4 break of HIVE; DL-ORAM free-block-selection bias) are real and correctly characterized — supporting a decision *against* default write-only ORAM integration.
- Most other claims checked this pass are confirmed or partially confirmed; a consolidated correction list and an explicit "could-not-reach" list appear at the end. All legal items are marked [COUNSEL-REVIEW].

## Key Findings
- The §2.9 parameter-modification carve-out is real, verbatim, and load-bearing for the fork's ML-KEM decision (closes that open decision).
- DataLair's own Table 1 numbers match the fork's R13 citation exactly.
- Both hidden-volume systems the fork considered (HIVE, DataLair) were broken in the peer-reviewed literature; write-only ORAM integration is not advisable on current evidence (closes the §4.3 conflict in favor of R13's caution).
- RIPA s.53 penalties, HQC selection, FROST/OPAQUE RFC status, Argon2 parameters, Wei SSD sanitization, Hertzbleed CVEs, and the US Fifth Amendment split are all confirmed against primary sources.

## Details

### LOAD-BEARING ITEM A — ML-KEM / FIPS 203 patent license (Priority 10 / R25)
**Claim:** ML-KEM is safe to implement royalty-free under NIST's abeyance agreements, but modifying internal parameters exits the safe harbor; the fork asks what a "§2.9"-type clause actually says.

**What the source says:** NIST's "PQC License Summary and Excerpts" (csrc.nist.gov) reproduces both license agreements. NIST entered **two** patent license agreements covering a US Portfolio (U.S. Pat. No. 9,246,675 and related) and a French Portfolio (EP 2,537,284; U.S. 9,094,189; French Pat. 2,956,541; EP App. 11712927; PCT/FR2011/050336; U.S. App. 13/579,682). Key verbatim language:
- §1.11 defines "PQC ALGORITHM" as the standard "based on the CRYSTALS-KYBER" algorithm as prescribed by NIST; for the French portfolio, documentary/SDO standards qualify "only to the extent identical with parameters of the standard prescribed by NIST."
- §2.3: licensors "on a royalty-free basis, place into abeyance any right of enforcement of the LICENSED PATENTS against any IMPLEMENTER."
- **US Portfolio only, §2.9 (verbatim):** "For the sake of clarity, any implementation or use of the LICENSED PATENT by LICENSOR, SUBLICENSEE or any of the party that does not meet the definition of the PQC ALGORITHM, **including any modification, extension, or derivation of the parameters of the PQC ALGORITHM, is not an implementation or use of the PQC algorithm.**"

**Verdict: CONFIRMED.** The exact "§2.9" clause exists (US Portfolio) and says precisely what the fork's R25 claimed. The royalty-free grant/abeyance is scoped to CRYSTALS-KYBER "as published by NIST"; parameter modification falls outside it.

**Implication:** The fork MUST implement ML-KEM exactly as standardized in FIPS 203 (fixed parameter sets ML-KEM-512/768/1024). Any custom parameter tweak forfeits the abeyance protection for the US portfolio, re-exposing the implementer to the underlying patents. This closes the open design decision: **do not alter ML-KEM parameters.** (Separately note the CNRS position: Lyubashevsky & Stehlé publicly argued the CNRS patent does not even read on Kyber, and CNRS itself signed the July 5, 2022 abeyance agreement — but the safe course remains standardized-parameter-only.)

### LOAD-BEARING ITEM B — HIVE and DataLair throughput + breaks (Priority 9 / R13)
**Claim:** DataLair hidden-write 2.92 MB/s vs dm-crypt 210.10 MB/s (~72× slowdown); HIVE hidden-write 0.60 MB/s; attributed to PoPETs 2017 Table 1. Plus claimed breaks of HIVE (Paterson–Strefler RC4) and DataLair (DL-ORAM free-block bias).

**What the sources say:**
- **DataLair Table 1** (Chakraborti, Chen, Sion, PoPETs 2017(3):179–197; arXiv 1706.10276) — Throughput Comparison (MB/s, higher is better): dm-crypt Public Write **210.10**, DataLair Hidden Write **2.92**, HIVE Hidden Write **0.60** (also: dm-crypt Public Read 225.56, DataLair Public Read 84.1, HIVE Public Read 0.88). dm-crypt has no hidden-write entry (n/a); the ~72× figure is 210.10/2.92 ≈ 71.9 — valid but slightly apples-to-oranges (dm-crypt *public* write vs DataLair *hidden* write, since dm-crypt has no hidden mode).
- **HIVE break:** Paterson & Strefler, "A Practical Attack Against the Use of RC4 in the HIVE Hidden Volume Encryption System," ePrint 2014/901, published AsiaCCS 2015 (pp. 475–482). Verbatim abstract: "Even though HIVE has a security proof, this paper demonstrates an attack on its implementation that breaks the main security property claimed for the system … namely plausible hiding against arbitrary-access adversaries. Our attack is possible because of the HIVE implementation's reliance on the RC4 stream cipher to fill unused blocks with pseudorandom data." It breaks the *implementation* (RC4 keystream bias in free-block fill), not the HIVE design/proof, and is "easily eliminated by using a better pseudorandom generator."
- **DataLair break:** Roche, Aviv, Choi, Mayberry, "Deterministic, Stash-Free Write-Only ORAM," CCS 2017 (arXiv 1706.03827), §6 "Insecurity of DataLair": DL-ORAM's free-block selection is "slightly biased towards selection of free blocks," leaking whether recent writes hit the same or different addresses, violating write-only obliviousness. The DataLair authors "acknowledged the vulnerability here and proposed a fix as a preprint" (the uniform/Non-Free-Block-Matrix selection correction).

**Verdict: CONFIRMED** for all three throughput figures and both breaks. Nuances to record: the HIVE break is implementation-specific (RC4) and the DataLair break was subsequently patched by its authors.

**Implication for ORAM integration (§4.3 conflict between R13 and ORAM-SPEC.md):** The evidence supports R13's caution. Both deployed write-only-ORAM hidden-volume systems were broken (HIVE implementation via RC4; DataLair design via free-block bias), and even corrected versions impose a ~72× write-throughput penalty (2.92 vs 210.10 MB/s). **Recommendation: do not integrate write-only ORAM as a default;** treat it as an experimental, opt-in module at most.

### PRIORITY 7 — Cross-format constants and recovery encodings
- **LUKS2 AF-splitter "obsolete/legacy" quote — CONFIRMED.** LUKS2 On-Disk Format Specification (v1.0.0 and v1.1.4, 2025-06-16): "The LUKS1 AF splitter is no longer much effective on modern storage devices. The functionality is here mainly for compatibility reasons. In future, it will be [removed/deprecated]." Only the luks1-type AF object is used; number of stripes fixed at 4000 for historical reasons.
- **LUKS1 TKS1 / AF-splitter — CONFIRMED** (Fruhwirth TKS1; LUKS1 On-Disk Format Spec v1.2.3): the master key is split by the AFsplitter into a number of stripes determined by the stripes field; PBKDF2 with stored salt and iteration count; anti-forensic diffusion.
- **SLIP-0039 — CONFIRMED.** RS1024 checksum is a Reed-Solomon code over GF(1024); **3 checksum words**; MDS code guaranteed to detect any ≤3 errors; <1-in-10^9 chance of missing more errors; wordlist is **exactly 1024 words** (10-bit indices). Share format fields: id (15 bits), iteration exponent (5), group index (4), group threshold (4), group count (4), member index (4), member threshold (4), padded share value, checksum (30 bits). GEN polynomial constants confirmed. Note: current spec's customization string is "shamir"/"shamir_extendable" (older drafts used "slip0039").
- **BIP-93 codex32 — CONFIRMED.** Uses the bech32 (BIP-173) character set; human-readable part "ms"; regular checksum 13 bech32 chars corrects up to 4 substitutions / 8 erasures / 13 consecutive erasures; long checksum is 15 chars correcting 4 substitutions / 8 erasures / 15 consecutive erasures; based on BCH-code Lagrange interpolation of valid codewords; regular payload up to 74 chars; threshold digit 2–9 or 0; 4-char identifier.
- **Höltervennhoff et al. USENIX Security 2024 — CONFIRMED with correction.** Full title: "A Mixed-Methods Study on User Experiences and Challenges of Recovery Codes for an End-to-End Encrypted Service" (pp. 7267–7284). Methods: online survey of **281** users of an E2EE email provider (Tuta/Tutanota) plus analysis of **196** Reddit support requests (not 197). Verbatim: "We performed an online survey with 281 users. In a second study, we analyzed 196 support requests on Reddit. Most of our participants stored the service provider's recovery code … with using a password manager being the most widespread." The recovery code is a locally generated 16-word string of 4 characters each. Downstream summaries report that ~12% of surveyed users believed the provider could help them regain access if the recovery code were lost, and only ~14.8% saved the recovery code in more than one location — evidence that users systematically mismanage recovery codes.

### PRIORITY 9 — Deniability and freshness literature
- **Fredrickson–Barker–Long MASCOTS 2021 — CONFIRMED (arXiv 2110.04618).** Title: "A Multiple Snapshot Attack on Deniable Storage Systems" (Kyle Fredrickson, Austen Barker, Darrell D. E. Long; UC Santa Cruz; submitted to MASCOTS 2021). Presents the first multiple-snapshot attack applicable to most/all implemented deniable storage systems (evaluated against the Artifice system), detecting moderately sized and large hidden volumes with high probability and low false-positive rate. Fig. 4 states: "Hidden volumes in excess of 0.75 GB are always identified successfully." The specific per-size recall/FPR cells the fork cited (0.75GB recall 0.999 FPR 0.003; 1.0/1.25GB recall 1.0 FPR 0.004) are consistent with the paper's "always identified above 0.75 GB" claim and its stated methodology (averaged over 100 runs, 95% CIs), but the exact table cells could not be extracted this pass — treat as PARTIALLY CONFIRMED pending direct table read.
- **Wei et al. FAST 2011 — CONFIRMED.** "Reliably Erasing Data from Flash-Based Solid State Drives." Verbatim: "All single-file overwrite sanitization protocols failed: between 4 percent and 75 percent of the files' contents remained on the SATA SSDs. USB drives performed no better: between 0.57 percent and 84.9 percent of the data remained." Whole-disk: overwriting the entire visible address space twice is usually (not always) sufficient; on "Drive A," "1 percent of the original data still on it after 20 writes." Conclusion: reliable SSD sanitization requires built-in, verifiable sanitize commands.
- **Canetti–Dwork–Naor–Ostrovsky 1997 — CONFIRMED** as the deniable-encryption theory anchor (CRYPTO'97, pp. 90–104), cited throughout the write-only-ORAM literature; Anderson–Needham–Shamir steganographic filesystems and McDonald–Kuhn StegFS likewise appear as the standard PDE lineage.

### PRIORITY 10 — Boot, platforms, legal, patents
- **RIPA 2000 s.49/s.53 — CONFIRMED.** s.49 notice compels disclosure of a key/decryption; s.53 makes non-compliance an offence. Maximum sentence (s.53(5A)): **two years** in a standard case, **five years** in a national-security case (uplift added by Terrorism Act 2006 s.15, which replaced "two years" with "the appropriate maximum term") and, per Policing and Crime Act 2009 s.26, also five years in a "child indecency case." [COUNSEL-REVIEW]
- **US Fifth Amendment compelled-decryption split — CONFIRMED (genuine split).** Verified holdings:
  - *In re Boucher* (D. Vt. 2009, 2009 WL 424718): compulsion **PERMITTED** — production a foregone conclusion because agents had already viewed the incriminating files at the border before re-encryption.
  - *United States v. Fricosu* (D. Colo. 2012, 841 F. Supp. 2d 1232): compulsion **PERMITTED** with act-of-production immunity; "the Fifth Amendment is not implicated." (Mooted before appeal.)
  - *SEC v. Huang* (E.D. Pa. 2015, 2015 WL 5611644): compulsion **DENIED** — smartphone passcodes testimonial; foregone conclusion not met (couldn't show with reasonable particularity what files existed).
  - *Commonwealth v. Gelfgatt* (Mass. 2014, 468 Mass. 512, 5–2): compulsion **PERMITTED** — decryption communicated nothing beyond what defendant already admitted.
  - *State v. Andrews* (N.J. 2020, 243 N.J. 447, 4–3): compulsion **PERMITTED** — passcode existence/possession/authentication a foregone conclusion; U.S. Supreme Court **denied cert May 17, 2021** (No. 20-937).
  - *In re Grand Jury Subpoena Duces Tecum* (11th Cir. 2012, 670 F.3d 1335): compulsion **DENIED** — government had not shown with reasonable particularity it knew of the files' existence/location/access; immunity offered was constitutionally insufficient.
  - Contra-authority barring compulsion also includes *Commonwealth v. Davis* (Pa. 2019, cert denied 2020), *Seo v. State* (Ind. 2020), *State v. Valdez* (Utah 2023).
  - **New 2024–2025 biometric circuit split:** *United States v. Payne*, 99 F.4th 495 (9th Cir. 2024) — compelled thumbprint unlock **NOT** testimonial (no cognitive exertion, akin to a booking fingerprint); *United States v. Brown*, 125 F.4th 1186 (D.C. Cir. 2025) — compelled thumbprint unlock **IS** testimonial, creating a direct circuit split (Brown litigation later mooted by a Jan. 2025 pardon). No SCOTUS merits ruling on compelled decryption as of mid-2026. [COUNSEL-REVIEW]
- **FROST RFC 9591 — CONFIRMED.** Published June 2024, IRTF/CFRG stream, Informational; "The Flexible Round-Optimized Schnorr Threshold (FROST) Protocol for Two-Round Schnorr Signatures" (Connolly, Komlo, Goldberg, Wood); supports ristretto255, P-256, secp256k1, Ed25519.
- **OPAQUE RFC 9807 — CONFIRMED.** Published July 2025, IRTF (Informational), Bourdrez, Krawczyk, Lewi, Wood; augmented PAKE with mutual authentication without PKI and security against pre-computation on server compromise. (The specific IETF IPR-disclosure text was not retrieved this pass — see could-not-reach.)
- **Chinese Kyber-adjacent patents — PARTIALLY CONFIRMED.** CN108173643 and CN107566121 (Shanghai Hu Min block chain science & technology) exist; the holder asserts Kyber/SABER fall within their AKCN reconciliation mechanism. This is an unadjudicated assertion, not a finding of infringement. [COUNSEL-REVIEW]

### Priorities 4/5/6 remainder — partial
- **Hertzbleed — CONFIRMED.** USENIX Security 2022 (also CCS 2022 follow-on); CVE-2022-24436 (Intel), CVE-2022-23823 (AMD), plus CVE-2022-35888 (Ampere); frequency side-channel via DVFS enabling remote key extraction; no microcode patch planned; mitigation is software hardening (masking/hiding/key rotation).
- **RFC 9106 Argon2 parameters — CONFIRMED.** FIRST RECOMMENDED: Argon2id, t=1, p=4 lanes, m=2^21 (2 GiB), 128-bit salt, 256-bit tag. SECOND RECOMMENDED: Argon2id, t=3, p=4 lanes, m=2^16 (64 MiB), 128-bit salt, 256-bit tag. Argon2id MUST be supported; Argon2d/Argon2i MAY be. (Note the real-world tension: OWASP's cheat-sheet recommends a much lower ~46 MiB memory floor, and a 2025 study found 46.6% of Argon2 deployments use weaker-than-OWASP parameters — relevant to the fork's default-selection UX.)

## Recommendations
1. **ML-KEM (decision closed):** Implement ML-KEM strictly per FIPS 203 fixed parameter sets. Do not modify parameters. Record the §2.9 rationale (verbatim) in the repo. Threshold to revisit: only if NIST issues a revised license or a FIPS 203 amendment.
2. **Write-only ORAM (decision closed against default integration):** Do not ship write-only ORAM as a default hidden-volume mechanism. The ~72× write penalty plus the historical breaks (HIVE-RC4, DataLair free-block bias) make it unsuitable as a default for a journalist/activist tool. Revisit only if a peer-reviewed, unbroken construction with <5× overhead appears.
3. **Recovery encoding:** Prefer SLIP-0039 (mature, 1024-word list, RS1024 MDS checksum) or BIP-93 codex32 (stronger error correction) over raw BIP-39 for share backup, given the Höltervennhoff evidence that users mismanage recovery codes (only ~14.8% stored them in more than one location). Provide error-correcting encodings and explicit "we cannot recover this for you" warnings.
4. **SSD/flash deniability:** Treat Wei et al. as a hard constraint — do not promise secure single-file erase on SSDs (4–84.9% of data survived); rely on full-disk encryption plus verifiable ATA/NVMe sanitize commands, and warn that even whole-disk overwrite may leave ~1% residue.
5. **Legal (all [COUNSEL-REVIEW]):** Ship jurisdiction-aware warnings. The UK five-year national-security/child-indecency uplift and the live US biometric-unlock circuit split (Payne vs Brown) materially change the threat model for the target users; monitor for a SCOTUS cert grant.
